Bulkley Watershed Fish Passage Restoration Planning 2022

Waterfall Creek - 198116 - Appendix

Site Location

PSCIS crossing 198116 is located on Waterfall Creek, in the town of New Hazelton, BC. PSCIS crossing 198116 is located on Highway 16. Waterfall Creek does not have accurate modelling data in the bcfishpass database. Any modelling included in this appendix was estimated using data collected in the field and GIS data. Crossing 198116 is located approximately 4km upstream from the confluence with Station Creek. Crossing 198116 is the responsibility of the Ministry of Transportation and Infrastructure.


Background

At crossing 198116, Waterfall Creek is a first order stream. Upstream of crossing 198116, cutthroat trout and dolly varden have previously been recorded (MoE 2020b; Norris 2020).


Waterfall Creek is a primary tributary of the Station/Waterfall Creek system. It’s located in the community of New Hazelton and is thus heavily impacted by urban and industrial development. A restoration project was carried out along Waterfall Creek in 2001 by Nortec Consulting. To help improve riparian vegetation, 300-400 seedlings were planted within 5 m of the creek. General stream enhancement work including the construction of walkways and bridges was also conducted (Consulting 2000). In 1990, the Chicago Creek Community Environmental Enhancement Society (CCES) developed a rehabilitation project that aimed to reintroduce coho salmon to Waterfall Creek using transplanted stocks from Toboggan Creek, near Smithers. After the transplant license ended, the project had to rely on salmon returning to the stream for brood stock to maintain population levels (Houlden and Donas 2002). Irvine (2021) conducted an assessment on two crossings located on tributaries to Waterfall Creek.


Habitat assessments were conducted on a tributary to Waterfall Creek by A. Irvine (2022) in 2021. Located upstream of PSCIS Crossing 198116, this tributary flows through New Hazelton and crosses the highway. The highway crossing and a crossing further downstream were assessed and both were deemed to be barriers at the time of survey, according to provincial protocols. A detailed background and habitat assessment can be found in the 2021 report here.


A map of the watershed is provided in map attachment .


Stream Characteristics at Crossing

At the time of the survey, PSCIS crossing 198116 was un-embedded, non-backwatered and ranked as a potential to upstream fish passage according to the provincial protocol (MoE 2011) (Table 2.18). Water temperature was 8\(^\circ\)C, pH was 8.1 and conductivity was 52uS/cm.

Stream Characteristics Downstream

The stream was surveyed downstream from crossing 198116 for 450m to the confluence with the Bulkley River (Figures 2.27 - 2.28). The dominant substrate was fines with gravels sub-dominant.Total cover amount was rated as abundant with overhanging vegetation dominant. Cover was also present as instream vegetation.The average channel width was 3m, the average wetted width was 2.8m, and the average gradient was 1%. There is a culvert approximately 100m upstream of PSCIS crossing 198116 that was also assessed. At the time of survey, this crossing was un-embedded, non-backwatered, and ranked as a barrier to upstream fish passage (Table 2.19).The stream flows through the town of New Hazelton. There was low flow at the time of survey and the stream contained a lot of waste and debris. There was a lot of cover from vegetation. The stream hits the railway and is drained into a tributary that flows west until it joins Station Creek. The habitat was rated as medium value for salmonid rearing and spawning.

Stream Characteristics Upstream

The stream was surveyed upstream from crossing 198116 for 1200m (Figures 2.29 - 2.30). The average channel width was 3.7m, the average wetted width was 3.5m, and the average gradient was 1.5%.Total cover amount was rated as abundant with small woody debris dominant. Cover was also present as undercut banks.Total cover amount was rated as abundant with overhanging vegetation dominant. Cover was also present as undercut banks.The dominant substrate was fines with gravels sub-dominant. There was a culvert approximately 400m upstream of PSCIS crossing 198116 that was also assessed. At the time of survey, this crossing was un-embedded, non-backwatered, and ranked as a barrier to upstream fish passage (Table 2.20). The habitat quality was excellent. The stream flow was moderate and there were fish spotted all the way up to the waterfall where the survey ended. There was an active minnow trap found with many fish inside. There was plenty of cover from vegetation and undercut banks. The habitat was rated as high value as an important migration corridor containing suitable spawning habitat and having moderate rearing potential.

Conclusion

There was 1.2km of habitat modelled upstream of crossing 198116 with areas surveyed rated as high value for salmonid rearing and spawning. There is a large impassable waterfall at the end of survey that blocks fish passage for all life stages. Crossing 198116 was ranked as a NA priority for proceeding to design for replacement.

The Waterfall Creek system is located directly in the community of New Hazelton. The stream is therefore significantly influenced by industrial and urban growth. There is a long history of community restoration and rehabilitation projects within the community. This crossing is an excellent candidate and should be replaced with an open bottom structure.
